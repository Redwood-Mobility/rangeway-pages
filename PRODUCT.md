# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Three audiences in priority order, with the first carrying tie-breaks on the homepage and shared sections:

1. **Hotel operators and property owners** evaluating whether to host a Rangeway on their land. They are reading the site the way a hospitality executive reads a brand deck: looking for evidence that this brand belongs on a premium property, not signage cluttering a parking lot. They make decisions in long horizons and care about brand fit, guest experience, and operator credibility.
2. **EV drivers** imagining a road trip with Rangeway in it. They want to feel that a stop is somewhere they'd want to be, not somewhere they have to be. They arrive on the site after hearing the brand from a club, a podcast, a peer, or a press piece, and they're forming a felt sense of what "Rangeway" means.
3. **Climatize-track and accredited investors** evaluating the Mojave raise and the broader thesis. They want to see a serious operator with a defensible position, not a deck dressed as a website.

Drivers and investors have dedicated surfaces (`/network`, `/our-story`, `/investors`, `/mojave`). The shared homepage and brand chrome lean operator-first.

## Product Purpose

Rangeway is America's first hospitality-driven EV charging network. The site exists to make that positioning legible at a glance and credible on inspection: hospitality DNA expressed through every detail, charging treated as a consequence of a great stop rather than the point of one. Success is operators saying "I'd host this," drivers saying "I'd plan a trip around this," and investors saying "this is a brand, not a utility."

## Brand Personality

**Warm, confident, hospitable.**

Voice is the voice of a senior hotelier, not a startup founder. Plainspoken, declarative, uncrowded. Specific over abstract. The warmth lives in materials, light, and place, not in voice tricks.

Lexicon is fixed and load-bearing:

- Formats: **Trailhead**, **Waystation**, **Basecamp**. Always capitalized as proper formats.
- Loyalty / experience: **Summit** (standalone, never "Rangeway Summit").
- Roles: **Trailkeepers**, **Lookouts**.
- Spaces: **driver's lounge** (always lowercase).
- Audience: **drivers**, **hotel operators**.

Hard prohibitions in copy: no em dashes, no `--`, no sentences starting with "And," no fragments, no hashtags, no statistics. No "stations." No "units" / "rooms" / "cabins" for formats.

## Anti-references

This must not look or feel like:

- **Tesla Supercharger and utility-aesthetic charging brands.** Cold, technical, parking-lot-coded. Treats the car as the customer.
- **Generic SaaS-cream startup landing pages.** Gradient hero, three-icon feature grid, "trusted by" logo strip, hero-metric template. The training-data reflex for any modern marketing site.
- **Crunchy outdoor / REI green-and-brown.** Rangeway lives outdoors but is hospitality, not gear. The aesthetic is a resort property in the high desert, not a trail map.
- **Ultra-luxe champagne-and-marble hotel sites.** Aloof, gilded, distant. Rangeway is warm and present, not unreachable.

The shared sin across all four: feeling abstract, generic, or category-coded. Rangeway should feel specific to itself.

## Design Principles

1. **Hospitality first, infrastructure second.** Every page treats the stop as the product, not the charger. Imagery and copy lead with people, light, and place; kilowatts and connectors are secondary, never the headline.
2. **Operator-credible polish.** Visual restraint and editorial pacing signal "this brand belongs on premium properties." When in doubt, fewer elements with more space.
3. **Warm, never cute.** Confidence without quirk. No emoji-driven personality, no lowercase-friend tone, no winking copy. The warmth is in materials, light, and place, not in voice tricks.
4. **Specific over generic.** "Waystation in Mojave," not "EV charging." Real place names, real amenities, real photography. The anti-references all share the sin of feeling abstract; specificity is the antidote.
5. **Quiet confidence over hype.** No statistics, no hashtags, no "trusted by" logos as a crutch. The product earns belief through clarity and craft, not claims.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- **WCAG 2.1 AA** as the floor across all surfaces.
- Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`: scroll-triggered reveals, scroll bobs, and any future motion must collapse to a static state.
- Maintain AA contrast on the warm-on-cream and warm-on-dark combinations even when warm is used at small scales (eyebrows, captions, links).
- Image alts must describe the scene's hospitality cue (light, materials, mood), not just the equipment. Hero example: "A Rangeway Waystation at dusk with sunset-lit mountains behind, lounge interior glowing through the glass facade, and a Rivian charging under the timber canopy."
- Light and dark schemes are both first-class. The dark mode flip in `global.css` is a real surface, not a fallback; copy and imagery must read on both.
